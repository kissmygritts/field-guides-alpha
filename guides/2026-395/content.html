Shooting Script · Solo · Late Aug – Early Sep

San Diego to Reno, the long way up 395

Three days east of the Sierra crest — desert tufa, living ghost towns, granite arches and dawn-lit alkali flats. Built to shoot the ends of the day and drive through the heat. No portraits, no LA, no Death Valley in September.

~530 mi total 3 nights camping Depart Fri 4:00 AM Route I-15 → CA-395 Golden hours are the whole point

San Diego → Lone Pine

Trona Pinnacles

Your marquee desert subject — 500+ tufa spires on a dry lakebed, straight out of a Shainblum frame. Access is ~5 mi of graded, washboarded dirt off CA-178; fine for a normal car if you crawl it. Beat the heat and the crowds: you want the low raking sun that separates each spire from its shadow. By 10:30 it flattens and bakes.

Low side-light, E/SE
Glass
24–70 + a longer lens to stack spires
Access
5 mi washboard dirt — crawl it
The shot Get low. Use a foreground spire as anchor, compress a cluster behind it with the longer lens so they overlap into a ridge of teeth. Shadows pointing at camera-left.

1.2 Late morning · ~11 AM

Randsburg

A living ghost town — wooden sidewalks, gold-rush facades, mining bones in the hills, ~100 residents still hanging on. This is your Nick Carver Americana chapter: hand-painted signage, sun-bleached siding, rust and patina. Detail and texture work, so midday flat light is fine here — you're not shooting sky.

Red Mtn & Jo'burg on the way
The shot One clean facade dead-on, symmetrical, filling the frame — then break it with a tight detail: a cracked sign, a padlock, boots of peeling paint. Shoot for a diptych.

1.3 Late morning · quick stop

Fossil Falls

A dry waterfall of water-polished black basalt — the ancient Owens River carved obsidian-smooth flutes, potholes and voids into a volcanic flow, with Red Hill cinder cone rising behind. A five-minute walk from the lot off US-395; almost nobody stops. Low raking light sculpts the rock best, but even midday hard shadow reads the strange scooped texture.

Light
Low & hard — rakes the flutes
Glass
24–70 + something close for texture
Access
Short graded dirt off US-395 — any car
The shot Get down into the rock: frame a smooth black void or pothole against the pale desert beyond, Red Hill cone as a distant anchor. Watch your footing — the drops are real.

1.5 Golden hour → camp

Alabama Hills

The payoff. Rounded granite boulders and natural arches with Mt. Whitney and the Sierra crest behind — one of the most-shot spots on the whole route, and it earns it. Mobius Arch is the classic (short walk from the Movie Rd trailhead) — it frames Whitney through the opening. Scout compositions in the good light, sleep here, shoot it again at dawn.

Light
Sunset warms boulders; dawn = alpenglow on crest
Glass
16–35 for arch foreground · tele for crest
Camp
Free dispersed on Movie Rd
The shot Mobius Arch low and close, wide lens, Whitney centered in the opening. Sunset for warm granite; set an alarm and come back for pink alpenglow at first light.
▲
Camp — Alabama Hills (free BLM dispersed, Movie Road) or Tuttle Creek CG nearby for a table + vault toilet. Arrive before dark to claim a spot and scout. Desert nights here are mild but the wind kicks up.

Lone Pine → June Lake

2.1 Early morning · reflective stop

Manzanarhistoric site

The WWII camp where the U.S. incarcerated over 10,000 Japanese-Americans — a National Historic Site right on US-395 between Lone Pine and Independence. Not a "shot" so much as a place you stop: the stone sentry posts and entrance sign, a reconstructed guard tower and barracks, the white cemetery monument standing against the Sierra wall. Drive the 3.2-mi auto tour; the museum in the old auditorium is worth an hour. Free.

Flat AM is fine — it's about content
Glass
35 / 50 · documentary, restrained
Time
45–90 min incl. museum
The shot The guard tower against the crest, or a stone sentry post framing the empty valley. Let it be quiet — restraint over drama does this place justice.

2.2 optional Morning · big detour

Ancient Bristlecone Pines+2–3 hrs

East of Bishop, a long climb to ~10,000 ft and the oldest trees on earth — twisted, sculptural, silver-and-amber against high desert. A Heaton patient-landscape subject, and cool even in summer thanks to the elevation. It's a committed out-and-back; make it a pace call. Before you leave Bishop, duck into the Mountain Light Gallery — Galen Rowell's life work of this exact landscape, and the best possible tuning-fork for your eye.

2.5 Midday · geothermal detour

Hot Creekgeologic site

A geothermal gorge where cobalt and turquoise hot springs boil up through a cold trout stream, banked in bleached mineral white and rust. Short paved road off US-395 just south of Mammoth, with an easy overlook and a trail down to the water. The colors punch hardest under high midday sun — a rare stop that rewards the flat light you're dodging everywhere else. No swimming: scalding and unpredictable.

Glass
24–70 + polarizer to cut glare
Access
Paved to lot — stay out of the water
The shot Shoot down the gorge, turquoise pools leading into the green creek with white mineral banks framing. Polarize to kill surface glare and saturate the blue.

The "Switzerland of California" — alpine lakes ringed by granite, on a scenic loop off 395. Aspens won't have turned yet this early, but the water, peaks and evening light carry it. Drive the loop, pick a west-facing shoreline, and shoot the last warm light on the crest reflecting off calm water.

Mono Lake — South Tufa

The signature Eastern Sierra frame: tufa spires rising straight out of a still, alien lake with the desert ranges behind. Build the whole morning around dawn here — colour behind the towers, glassy surface before the wind, the place nearly empty. South Tufa reserve, small fee. Get in position in the dark.

3.3 Midday · big detour

Bodie+2–3 hrs

The best-preserved ghost town in the West — 100+ weathered buildings held in official "arrested decay," a gold-rush town frozen mid-collapse at 8,375 ft. Turn east off US-395 onto Hwy 270; the last 3 mi are rough dirt (fine dry, any vehicle). Rusted autos in the sage, the leaning Methodist church, the Standard Mill, interiors left exactly as abandoned. Flat light is no enemy here — it's texture and story, not golden hour. Small day-use fee, cash.

Field notes

Heat is the spine of the plan

Shoot the desert in Friday's cool early hours, drive the midday heat while gaining elevation, and by Saturday night you're camped at 7,000+ ft. Bring a real warm layer — the east side drops fast after dark even in summer.

Water & fuel

Long gaps between services on 395. Top off at Ridgecrest, Bishop, Lee Vining. Carry more water than you think for the desert day — there's none at Trona.

The one reservation

Sat night around June Lake / Mammoth is competitive in summer. Book ahead or pin a dispersed FS backup. Days 1 & 3 camping is open and easy.

Why not Death Valley

Early September routinely runs 110°F+ — miserable and unsafe to camp. Trona gives you the same tufa/dry-lakebed look without the oven. Good call skipping it.

Dirt-road stops

Trona (5 mi), Crowley Columns, Cerro Gordo — all graded/rough dirt. Crawl the washboard, watch clearance, and don't attempt anything soft after rain.

Rhythm

Two golden hours a day is the target: dawn + dusk. Midday is for driving and the flat-light detail work (Randsburg, bristlecone grain). Don't burn energy shooting harsh noon landscapes.
