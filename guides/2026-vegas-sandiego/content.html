Shooting Script · Overland · Mid-October

Las Vegas to San Diego, the back way

Two days southwest that barely touch a freeway. Skip I-15 entirely: drop down US-95 into the volcanic heart of the Mojave National Preserve, run a forgotten stretch of Route 66, cross the badlands of Anza-Borrego, then climb the pine crest above San Diego and coast down after dark. Built around the ends of the day, with dark desert nights between.

~600 mi total 2 nights · Preserve + 29 Palms Depart Fri PM Route no I-15 Waxing-crescent moon · dark late

Day 1 · Saturday · Preserve & Route 66

Vegas → Amboy → Twentynine Palms

Slip out of Vegas Friday night, camp in the Mojave Preserve, and spend Saturday working its volcanic middle — a granite dome, a red-rock slot, a mission depot, booming dunes — then drop onto Route 66 for a cinder cone and the loneliest neon in California. ~230 mi.

Fri PM depart Vegas · US-95 S dawn Cima Dome AM Hole-in-the-Wall midday Kelso Depot + Dunes pm Amboy Crater dusk Roy's → 29 Palms
⛽ Fuel Fill up in Las Vegas — the Preserve has no gas at all. Next reliable pumps are Baker or Ludlow, then Twentynine Palms. 📵 Signal Dead across the entire Preserve and Route 66. Download maps + this guide before you leave Vegas. 💧 Water Carry 6+ L each — no potable water at any Preserve stop, and October still hits ~90°F midday on the low flats. 🚗 Drive ~230 mi · much of it graded dirt · 5–6 hr moving + stops
Granite dome · Joshua trees · first light
1.1 optional Dawn · first frame

Cima Dome & Teutonia PeakMojave Preserve

A near-perfect granite dome — 75 square miles of it, rising so symmetrically it reads as almost engineered — with the boulder-stacked Teutonia Peak trail picking through what was the world's densest Joshua-tree forest. Honest note: the 2020 Dome Fire burned much of that forest, so today you're shooting survivors and silvered snags among the monzogranite, not the endless green army it was. Still a quiet, strange place to open the trip. Low sun rakes the boulders and backlights the Joshuas that made it.

Light
First light · low sun backlighting the Joshuas
Glass
24–70 · wide to set the dome's curve
Access
Paved Cima Rd + short trail · fire-scarred
The shot A lone surviving Joshua tree rim-lit at sunrise, stacked granite boulders behind it, the dome's clean arc on the horizon. Then a stark minimal — a single burned snag against boulder and sky.
Red volcanic rock · pockets · iron rings
1.2 Morning

Hole-in-the-Wall

Rust-red volcanic rock pocked with hundreds of wind-and-gas hollows — debris from a violent eruption 18 million years ago. The Rings Trail drops you down through a slot in the rhyolite on iron rings bolted to the stone, walls closing overhead into Banshee Canyon. The oxidized tuff glows when low sun hits it. One of the Preserve's most graphic, texture-dense stops — Swiss-cheese walls from every angle.

Light
Low sun ignites the red rock · flat is fine for the pockets
Glass
16–35 in the slot · 70–200 for wall texture
Access
Graded dirt (Black Canyon Rd) + ring scramble
The shot Down inside Banshee Canyon: pocked red walls curving around you, the iron rings or a figure for scale. Then tight abstracts of the tafoni — pockets within pockets, warm on cool.
Mission depot · palms · Americana
1.3 Midday · roadside

Kelso Depot1924 · restored

A gleaming white Spanish-mission railroad depot marooned in the middle of the desert — red-tile roof, arcaded veranda, built 1924 for the Union Pacific and now the Preserve's visitor center. Palms, a long empty platform, freight trains still grinding past on the mainline. Pure Americana: architectural symmetry against raw desert, the Providence Mountains behind. Works even under flat midday light — you're shooting the building, not the sky.

Light
Front light on the white facade AM · warm side-light PM
Glass
24–70 · symmetry, arches, receding veranda
Access
Paved (Kelbaker Rd) — any car
The shot The depot dead-on and symmetrical, palms framing, red tile against blue. Then the arcade receding down the veranda in hard shadow — and hold for a freight stack blurring past behind it.
Rose-gold dunes · knife-edge crests · form
1.4 Afternoon → golden hour

Kelso Dunes

Six hundred feet of rose-gold sand piled into one of the tallest dune fields in the Mojave — and when you slide down the steep lee faces they boom, a low aircraft-engine drone from the shearing sand. A short walk off the trailhead puts you onto clean, unfootprinted ridgelines. This is your pure-form chapter: line, shadow, ripple, nothing else. Save it for the last hour, when raking light cuts the crests into knife-edges.

Light
Last hour — raking light for ridgelines & ripples
Glass
70–200 to compress crests · wide for the sweep
Access
Graded dirt to TH + ~1.5 mi soft-sand walk
The shot A single sinuous crest dividing light from shadow, wind ripples leading in, not a footprint in frame. Go long to stack ridge on ridge; wait out the last low sun for the deepest shadow.
Cinder cone · black lava sea · symmetry
1.5 Late afternoon

Amboy CraterRoute 66

A textbook 250-ft cinder cone sitting alone on a black lava field beside Route 66, last erupted maybe 10,000 years ago. A trail crosses the basalt to a breach in the west wall and climbs to the rim; all around, the flow is a dead-flat sea of black rock. Shoot it late — low sun models the cone's symmetry and throws the lava field into long shadow. Midday here is brutal and flat, so don't waste that light on it.

Light
Late afternoon models the cone · skip midday
Glass
24–70 · wide for the lava sea, tele to isolate the cone
Access
Paved lot off Route 66 + exposed lava trail
The shot The symmetrical cone across the black flow, warm rim-light, a thread of trail leading in for scale. Then get low and shoot the cracked basalt into the sun for texture and a sun-star.
Googie neon · Mother Road · blue hour
1.6 Dusk · blue hour

Roy's Motel & Caféthe sign

The great googie boomerang sign of Roy's — Amboy's 1950s Route 66 outpost, population near zero, the most photographed neon in the Mojave. The soaring pylon, the shuttered café, a rusted gas pump, and the dead-straight Mother Road running off to a vanishing point. They relight the neon on occasion; even dark, the sign against a blue-hour or star-filled sky is the frame. This is your last stop before the run down Amboy Road to Twentynine Palms.

Light
Blue hour into full dark · neon or stars behind the pylon
Glass
24–70 + wide · tripod for the dark frames
Access
Paved, right on Route 66
The shot The boomerang pylon dead-center against a deep post-sunset blue, Route 66 leading straight to it. Later, if it's dark and clear, the sign silhouetted under stars. A rusted pump detail for the diptych.
▲
Overnight — Twentynine Palms (motels + the Joshua Tree gateway), or dispersed on Amboy Road / back in the Preserve if you'd rather stay under stars. Fuel up and eat before you turn in — Sunday is the long day. The crescent moon sets early, so if it's clear, walk out from the sign for stars before you go.

Day 2 · Sunday · Badlands to the Pines

29 Palms → Anza-Borrego → San Diego

The long day. Traverse Joshua Tree at dawn, take the one unavoidable freeway hop, then drop into the Anza-Borrego badlands — a Grand-Canyon overlook and a slot canyon — before climbing the switchbacks to apple-pie Julian and running the pine crest down into San Diego after dark. ~250 mi.

Cholla backlight · granite jumbles · dawn
2.1 optional Dawn · traverse

Joshua Treenorth → south traverse

Enter at Twentynine Palms and drive the park's spine south to Cottonwood — the fastest low-road link toward the coast, and a worthy first frame either way. Up high: the namesake trees and monzogranite jumbles around Jumbo Rocks and Skull Rock. Dropping into the Pinto Basin, the Cholla Cactus Garden lights up like fur when low sun backlights ten thousand spines. Shoot the cholla at first light, grab the rocks on the way through, then keep moving — the day is long.

Light
First light backlighting the cholla · long shadows in the rocks
Glass
24–70 · macro / tele for cholla halos
Access
Paved park road end-to-end · entrance fee
The shot The Cholla Garden backlit at sunrise, every spine a halo, one plant isolated against shadowed hills. Up in the rocks: a Joshua tree against a granite pile, hard dawn shadow raking across.
Sandstone slot · rock bridge · soft light
2.2 Midday · flat-light friendly

The SlotAnza-Borrego

A narrow sandstone slot you walk down into — walls pinching to shoulder-width overhead, a natural rock bridge spanning the gap, soft light bouncing down sculpted mudstone. Unlike the badland overlooks, a slot shoots best under flat midday sun, when top light filters down without blowing the rim — so this is the stop to drop into when you roll in around noon. Short, easy, out of the wind, and completely unlike anything else on the drive.

Light
Midday soft top-light is ideal · avoid hard low sun
Glass
16–35, vertical · the walls do the composing
Access
Sandy access road + short walk · never in flash-flood weather
The shot Vertical, looking up the pinched walls to a ribbon of sky, the rock bridge crossing overhead. Then a tight study of the wind-carved flutes where the bounced light goes warm.
Badland labyrinth · cliff edge · raking light
2.3 Late afternoon · raking light

Font's PointAnza-Borrego

"California's Grand Canyon" — an abrupt cliff-edge over the Borrego Badlands, a chaos of eroded mudstone ridges and washes falling away toward the Salton basin. Raw and enormous. Low, raking light carves the badland fins into three dimensions; harsh overhead sun flattens them to mush — so if you got here midday, do The Slot first and circle back for the late side-light. The four miles in are a soft-sand wash: air down, 4WD advised, and don't attempt it if rain threatens.

Light
Sunset (or sunrise) rakes the ridges · midday kills it
Glass
24–70 wide at the rim · tele to isolate the fins
Access
4 mi soft-sand wash · high-clearance 4WD, air down
The shot Standing at the rim, wide, the whole badland maze raking away in low side-light. Then go long and pull single eroded fins out of the chaos, layer stacked on layer.
Switchbacks · valley overlook · blue shadow
2.4 optional Golden hour · on the climb

Montezuma GradeS-22 climb

The road out of the basin. County S-22 claws 3,000 feet up from the Borrego floor to Ranchita in a run of switchbacks, and the pullouts near the top — the Crawford Overlook especially — open onto the whole Borrego Valley, its badlands, and the Salton Sea beyond. It's at its best when the setting sun sidelights the ridges and the valley floods with blue shadow. Time your climb for golden hour and stop at the rail.

Light
Sunset — valley in warm side-light, shadows pooling
Glass
24–70 · tele to compress the ridgelines into layers
Access
Paved (S-22) · paved pullouts — any car
The shot From the overlook, the switchback road threading down into the vast valley, low sun raking the floor, the distant Salton Sea catching the last light. Or a tele compression of ridgelines going blue with haze.
False fronts · string lights · pine air
2.5 Dusk · blue hour

Julianmountain town

A gold-rush mountain town gone to apples and pie, at 4,235 ft in the Cuyamacas — false-front main street, wood boardwalks, cider mills, pine air. Mid-October is peak apple season, so it'll be busy and golden: warm-lit windows, string lights, woodsmoke at dusk. A complete change of key from the desert two hours behind you. Shoot the main street at blue hour, when the storefronts glow against the deepening sky.

Light
Blue hour — lit storefronts against deep sky
Glass
35 / 50 · signage, warm windows, boardwalk detail
Access
Paved (CA-78/79) — any car · busy in fall
The shot Main Street at blue hour: warm false-front stores, a hand-painted or neon sign, string lights overhead. Put a slice of pie in the frame if you want the cliché — at this point you've earned it.
Pine crest · desert overlook · dark sky
2.6 After dark · the descent

Sunrise HighwayMt. Laguna

The last act. County S-1 runs the crest of the Laguna Mountains at 5,000–6,000 ft through Jeffrey pine and black oak, the east side dropping in sheer overlooks straight down to the Anza-Borrego desert you just crossed. In daylight the Desert View and Stephenson Peak pullouts are staggering; by the time you reach them it may be full dark — so make it your dark-sky window. The crescent moon has set, and the desert glitters far below before the long coast down into San Diego and the city lights.

Light
Overlooks at last light · then stars over the desert rim
Glass
24–70 · tripod for the dark descent + any night frame
Access
Paved (S-1) · genuinely cold at 6,000 ft after dark
The shot If you catch last light: the pine ridge dropping thousands of feet to the desert floor, backlit. After dark: a long exposure of stars and distant desert lights from a pullout, pines framing — then the switchbacks down to the coast.
◆
Final run: S-1 down off the mountains to I-8, then west into San Diego for Sunday night — arriving well after dark, salt air finally replacing desert dust. Roughly 600 miles and two golden days out of Las Vegas, and you barely touched a freeway the whole way.

Field notes

Skipping I-15 by design

The direct Vegas–San Diego shot is I-15, four dull hours of freeway. This route trades that for US-95 → Mojave Preserve → Route 66 → Amboy Rd → Joshua Tree → Anza-Borrego → Julian → Sunrise Hwy, with a single unavoidable ~35-mi stint on I-10. Longer and slower on purpose — the point is what's between the dots.

Fuel & signal

Long dead legs. The Mojave Preserve has no fuel and no signal at all — fill in Vegas, download everything first. Top off again at Twentynine Palms, on I-10, and in Borrego Springs. Julian is your last mountain gas before the coast.

Dirt & 4WD

Your rig earns its keep here. Font's Point is 4 miles of soft-sand wash — air down, 4WD, and never in the rain. Kelso Dunes and Hole-in-the-Wall are graded dirt; the Preserve backroads are washboard but fine dry. Check for flash-flood weather before any wash or slot.

Heat, then real cold

Mid-October is kind but split: desert floors (Amboy, Anza-Borrego) still push past 90°F midday — shoot them at the ends of the day, carry more water than you think. Then Julian and Mt. Laguna sit at 4,000–6,000 ft and go cold after dark. Pack for both in the same day.

Dark skies, quiet moon

Oct 16–18 is a waxing crescent (~28–49% lit) that sets not long after sunset — so the late nights go properly black in the Preserve and in Anza-Borrego (an International Dark-Sky park). The catch: by mid-October the Milky-Way core sets soon after dusk, so it's more a stars-and-foreground night than a core session.

The long Sunday

Day 2 is the big one — a dawn start in Joshua Tree and a headlamp arrival in San Diego. Don't over-stack it: The Slot and Font's Point are the anchors, Montezuma and the Sunrise Hwy overlooks are drive-through wins. If you're running late, cut Font's and keep the golden-hour climb.
