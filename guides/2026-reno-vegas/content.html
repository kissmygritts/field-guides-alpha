Shooting Script · Solo · Late September

Reno to Las Vegas, down the empty middle

Two days south on US-95 through the loneliest basins in Nevada — a cobalt desert lake, a valley of ammunition bunkers, three ghost towns and a forest of buried cars. Built around the ends of the day, with a full moon to light the ruins after dark. Not much between the dots — that's the point.

~450 mi total 1 night Tonopah Depart Sat AM Route US-95 S Golden hours + a full moon

Day 1 · Saturday · The High-Desert Run

Reno → Tonopah

Drop out of Reno onto US-95 and run the empty basins south. A river-fort ruin, a big blue lake, a valley of bunkers — then land in Tonopah for the mining town and a moonlit night at 6,000 ft. ~255 mi.

AM depart Reno · US-95 S opt Fort Churchill midday Walker Lake pm Hawthorne bunkers dusk Tonopah
⛽ Fuel Fallon, Hawthorne, Tonopah — long empty gaps between. Top off every chance. 📵 Signal Dead across most of US-95. Download maps + this guide before you leave. 💧 Water Carry 4+ L — nothing roadside at Walker Lake or the bunkers. 🚗 Drive ~255 mi · 4.5–5 hr moving
Adobe ruins · cottonwoods · low walls
1.1 optional Morning · quick warm-up

Fort Churchillstate park

An 1860s U.S. Army cavalry post melting back into the desert — roofless adobe walls ranged along the Carson River, cottonwoods behind them, a small pioneer cemetery. A short detour off the Alt US-95 corridor near Silver Springs, almost nobody there. This is your Nick Carver warm-up: earth-colored walls, texture, restraint. Morning side-light rakes the eroded adobe best.

Light
Low AM side-light on the walls
Glass
35 / 50 · documentary, restrained
Access
Paved park road — any car · small fee
The shot One adobe wall raking away from camera, warm light catching the crumbling edges, cottonwoods and empty sky behind. Then a tight detail of eroded mud brick.
Cobalt water · barren range · minimal
1.2 Midday · roadside

Walker Lake

A startling deep-blue desert lake with the barren Wassuk Range dropping straight into it — US-95 runs for miles right along the eastern shore, so it comes to you. Heaton-style minimalism: water, ridge, sky, nothing else. Pull off at Sportsman's Beach or any of the shoulder turnouts. The blue punches hardest under high sun; a polarizer deepens it.

Light
Any · high sun saturates the blue
Glass
Wide for emptiness · long to stack ridge on water
Access
Paved turnouts right off US-95
The shot Horizon on the lower third, one clean band of cobalt water under the bare range. Polarize to kill glare and saturate. Or go long and compress the ridge onto the waterline.
Endless grid · surreal · repetition
1.3 optional Afternoon · roadside only

Hawthorne Army Depotshoot from the road

The world's largest ammunition depot — thousands of turf-covered concrete bunkers in a dead-flat grid, marching to the horizon on both sides of US-95. Utterly surreal, endlessly graphic, and free from the car window. It's an active military base: do not enter or approach the fence. Everything you want is a compression shot from public road shoulders. High flat light actually helps the repetition read.

Light
Flat is fine · low sun rakes the mounds
Glass
70–200 · compress the grid into pattern
Access
Roadside only — no entry, active base
The shot Long lens straight down a row so the igloos stack into an infinite repeating rhythm. Keep a lone bunker off-center to break the grid. No fences, no signage in frame.
Headframes · moonlit · silver-boom bones
1.4 Blue hour → moonlit night

Tonopah

A silver-boom town frozen halfway to ghost — and the natural midpoint of the whole drive. The Historic Mining Park puts weathered headframes and hoist works on the hill directly above Main Street; the Clown Motel and the old cemetery sit side by side at the north end; the Mizpah Hotel anchors downtown. Tonopah is famous for some of the darkest skies in the country — but you're here on a full moon, so lean into it: silvered mine timbers and empty streets under moonlight instead of the core.

Light
Blue hour on the headframes · moonlight after dark
Glass
24–70 · tripod for dusk + moonlit long exposures
Stay
Mizpah, Clown Motel, or dispersed BLM outside town
The shot A headframe stark against the deep blue-to-black sky just after sunset, town lights beginning below. Later: the mining park bones lit only by the full moon, long exposure, hard shadows.
▲
Overnight — Tonopah (Mizpah or Clown Motel in town, or free dispersed BLM just outside). Your highest, coldest night at ~6,000 ft — pack a real warm layer even in September. Grab fuel and any food before you turn in; nothing's open early on the road south.

Day 2 · Sunday · Ghost Towns & Blue Water

Tonopah → Las Vegas

The good stuff, packed into the southern half: two ghost towns, a forest of buried cars, an optional Death Valley overlook and a turquoise desert oasis — then the casino lights. ~210 mi + detours.

AM Goldfield AM Car Forest midday Rhyolite · Goldwell opt Zabriskie Point pm Ash Meadows eve Las Vegas

Living ghost town · rust · silvered wood
2.1 Early morning · first stop

Goldfield

Once the biggest city in Nevada, now ~250 people among the bones of a gold rush. The grand shuttered Goldfield Hotel, the stone courthouse, the Santa Fe Saloon, rusted autos and mine headframes scattered through the sage. This is your Americana chapter — hand-painted signage, sun-bleached siding, rust and patina. Flat morning light is fine; you're shooting texture and story, not sky.

Light
Flat OK · low sun for wood grain
Glass
35 / 50 · tight on texture & signage
Note
Buildings are private/fragile — shoot from public ground
The shot The Goldfield Hotel dead-on, symmetrical, filling the frame — then break it with a tight detail: a cracked sign, a rusted fender, peeling paint on siding. Shoot for a diptych.
Buried cars · graffiti · Mad-Max sculpture
2.2 Morning · quick & strange

Int'l Car Forest of the Last Churchfree · open

On the scrub at the west edge of Goldfield: 40+ cars, buses and a limo planted nose-down and stacked into towers, every panel covered in spray-paint art. Cadillac Ranch by way of Mad Max. Free, always open, a graded-dirt lot — walk right in among them. Wild in any light; golden hour sets the paint and rust glowing, and it'd be eerie under the full moon if you're still nearby after dark.

Light
Golden hour on the paint · harsh sun works too
Glass
16–35 · get in among them, shoot up
Access
Short graded dirt — fine dry · pack out trash
The shot Low and wide beneath a planted car so it looms against sky, graffiti reading edge-to-edge. Then a tight abstract of peeling paint on rusted steel. Sun-star behind a tower at golden hour.
Boomtown ruin · ghost figures · sunset
2.3 Late afternoon → sunset

Rhyolite & Goldwellthe marquee

The payoff. A 1900s boomtown that emptied almost as fast as it filled, just off NV-374 near Beatty: the three-story Cook Bank shell, the glass-walled Bottle House, the old train depot standing alone in the flats. At the entrance, the Goldwell Open Air Museum — a cluster of ghostly white plaster figures ("The Last Supper") staring out over the desert. Sunset warms the ruins; then the ghosts glow under the full moon.

Light
Sunset on the ruins · moonlight on the white figures
Glass
24–70 + wide · foreground ghost, ruin behind
Access
Paved to both · free · open dusk-to-dawn
The shot Cook Bank's empty windows against a burning sky, low sun through the openings. Then a shrouded Goldwell figure close and low at blue hour, ruin or moon behind it. Come back after full dark for the plaster to go luminous.
Golden badlands · dawn only · heat
2.4 optional Dawn only · big detour

Zabriskie PointDeath Valley

If you overnight in Beatty instead and can make a dawn run, Death Valley's most famous overlook is ~30 min west on NV-374/CA-190: rippling golden badlands below the sharp spine of Manly Beacon, the whole basin catching first light. It's a classic for a reason. The catch is heat — late September still routinely hits 100°F+ by mid-morning here, so this is a get-in-at-sunrise-and-leave stop, not a midday one.

Light
Sunrise only — first light sets the badlands glowing
Glass
24–70 + tele to isolate the eroded ridgelines
Heat
100°F+ by mid-AM · dawn dash, carry water
The shot Manly Beacon lit warm against the shadowed folds below, sun just cresting. Go long to pull out the erosion patterns, or wide to set the ridge into the whole basin. Be gone before the heat.
Turquoise springs · desert oasis · mirror
2.5 Afternoon · last frame

Ash Meadowswildlife refuge

An improbable desert oasis in the Amargosa hardpan near the California line — turquoise spring pools so clear they look tropical, fed by fossil water, with boardwalks over them and the Funeral Mountains behind. Crystal Reservoir mirrors the ranges; Devils Hole (the fenced blue slot that's home to one tiny endangered fish) is a short drive on. A cool, saturated closer before the run into Vegas. Midday high sun makes the blues glow.

Light
High sun saturates the spring blue · calm = reflection
Glass
24–70 + polarizer · boardwalks as lead-in lines
Access
Some graded-dirt refuge roads — fine dry · stay on boardwalks
The shot Shoot down into a spring, turquoise water against pale desert, a boardwalk edge leading in. Polarize to cut surface glare and push the blue. At Crystal Reservoir, center the ridge on its own reflection.
◆
Final run: US-95 S through Amargosa Valley and Indian Springs into Las Vegas for Sunday evening. The desert stays wide and empty almost to the casino glow — if the light's still working near Indian Springs, that Joshua-tree flat is the sleeper frame of the drive.

Field notes

Full moon, not dark skies

Late Sept 2026 lands on a full moon (Sep 26), not the new moon — so this isn't a Milky-Way trip. Lean into it: moonlit ghost towns, silvered mine timbers and desert lit like day. If the core is the goal, shift ~2 weeks earlier or ~1 week later and the same route goes properly dark.

Fuel & signal

US-95 is genuinely empty. Top off at Fallon, Hawthorne, Tonopah and Beatty — long legs between, and no cell signal across most of it. Download maps and this guide before you leave pavement you know.

Heat by latitude

The northern half is mild and the Tonopah night is cold at 6,000 ft — bring a warm layer. The southern low stops (Death Valley, Ash Meadows) still bake past 100°F; shoot them early and carry more water than you think.

Ghost-town etiquette

Rhyolite and Goldfield are fragile and partly private — no trespassing, take nothing. Rhyolite is BLM "arrested decay." The Car Forest is free and open but has no services: pack everything out.

Hawthorne is an active base

The bunker field is stunning but it's a live Army depot. Shoot only from public road shoulders — no entering, no approaching the fence, and keep gates and signage out of frame.

Rhythm

Two golden hours plus a moonlit night is the target. Midday is for driving and flat-light texture work — Goldfield siding, the bunker grid, Walker Lake's saturated blue. Don't burn energy on harsh-noon landscapes.
